Consulting

My services are available for free for University of Arizona Health Sciences groups, through my current role as a UA Data Science Fellow. I can:

  • Help make fuzzy data or task requirements more crisp
  • Act as a guide through the maze of data visualization and wrangling software possibilities
  • Run visualization training or creativity workshops
  • Build custom creative software, visual analytics systems, data wrangling tools, or other advanced web applications

Please note that I tend to decline or deflect requests to collaborate unless they're a good fit. Please don't take it personally if I seem hesitant—if working with me will waste your time, I'll do my best to let you to know right away.

What makes a "good fit?"

The most important indicator to me that a collaboration will go well is access. Not enough access means that your project probably isn't quite ready for someone like me yet, and I'll probably just steer you toward relevant resources to help you get there.

Access to people

Do people in your organization already have some of the roles on this page? Great! How much of their time can they afford to spend meeting with me?

It's especially important for me to have access to the real users and consumers of any systems or visualizations that we create: just as unit tests are critical to guarantee bug-free software, interviews and user testing are critical to guarantee that software will be useful.

My first objective—both in terms of order and priority—is to create and update an evolving design document that details diverse stakeholder needs. I view software prototypes and systems as secondary means to better understand and achieve the needs of people, not ends in and of themselves. This often results in throwing code out, rewriting it, or building new systems from scratch. The sunk cost fallacy can be really strong in these projects, but I don't view discarding months of coding as a failure if the code taught us something that changed the design document.

Access to data

How much time will it take for me to get access to real data?

Real data behavior is almost always different from what you expect, and it's usually different enough that it breaks any early visualization designs that we might imagine. But, if the context and goals of the project are well-understood, the way that data breaks things often inspires truly innovative visualization designs: "necessity is the mother of invention" definitely applies to working with data.

Without understanding what necessities the data are imposing, it's a well-documented phenomenon that the lack of real data can spell serious trouble for a project. If you're still working on collecting data, or still thinking about what data you should collect, this isn't necessarily a deal-breaker—but we'll need to be especially careful early on to establish a healthy culture of data review.
